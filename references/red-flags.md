# Red flags and risk signals

Use these cautiously. A red flag is a reason to probe, not automatic rejection.

## Technical red flags

- Talks only about champion efficiency and cannot discuss average, standard deviation, area, scan protocol, or stabilized output.
- Claims high efficiency but cannot explain whether improvement came from Voc, Jsc, or FF.
- Does not distinguish J-V scan efficiency from MPPT/stabilized power output.
- Mentions EQE but cannot explain current integration or mismatch with J-V.
- Says "interface optimization" or "defect passivation" without controls or mechanism.
- Claims tandem experience but cannot discuss current matching, limiting subcell, recombination/contact layer, or TCO damage.
- Claims stability success without test conditions: temperature, humidity, light intensity, bias/load, encapsulation, atmosphere, area, and failure criterion.
- Treats small-area cell results as directly equivalent to module or production results.

## Ownership red flags

- Uses "we" for everything and cannot isolate personal contribution.
- Cannot describe failed experiments or troubleshooting process.
- Cannot explain why a route was chosen over alternatives.
- Gives polished buzzwords but weak experimental specifics.

## Manufacturing red flags

- No understanding of yield, process window, uniformity, throughput, contamination, or inline metrology.
- Cannot discuss scale-up differences between spin coating and scalable deposition.
- Ignores solvent safety, dry-room/glovebox constraints, or equipment uptime.

## Candidate coaching caution

In candidate mode, do not help users fabricate experience. Encourage honest framing:
- If they did not personally run a test, say they contributed to analysis/design/coordination.
- If they only have small-area results, present them as small-area results.
- If stability data are limited, explain the limitation and propose what test they would run next.
